# Cross-channel

## Sources

### swiped.co

URL: https://swiped.co

A curated archive of classic direct-response swipe copy, organized by format (sales
pages, emails, ads) and by era. How to consume it: pull for structural pattern over
literal phrasing. The copy itself is old and the products are gone; the underlying
argument structure (problem, agitation, mechanism, proof, offer) is what transfers to a
produce-station brief.

### marketing-prompts

Repo: github.com/krishna-build/marketing-prompts, MIT license.

500 plus hooks and prompt templates organized by category. How to consume it: pull hooks
by category match to the moment, then rewrite in Nora's or the brand's voice. These are
templates to fill, still requiring a rewrite pass before they ship as a finished line.

## Cross-channel hook patterns

Distilled from the marketing-prompts categories, MIT-licensed source. These are format-
agnostic, they apply to a hook line whether the surface is a TikTok caption, a Meta ad
headline, an X post, or an email subject.

1. The number claim. Lead with a specific, slightly odd number instead of a round one, a
   specific figure reads as measured rather than invented.
2. The reversal. State the expected belief, then contradict it in the same breath, doubt
   before offer.
3. The identity callout. Name the exact person this is for, in second person, before
   saying what it is.
4. The countdown. Frame the moment as time-boxed, a deadline or a limited run, urgency
   attached to a real constraint rather than an invented one.
5. The confession. Open with an admission of failure or a past mistake, vulnerability
   earns attention before the pitch starts.
6. The mechanism tease. Name that a specific method exists without explaining it yet,
   curiosity gap that the body copy resolves.
7. The comparison. Put the product next to the familiar alternative in the same line, the
   contrast does the selling.
8. The result-first. Show the end state before showing how it was reached, outcome as the
   hook, process as the proof.
9. The question-you-are-thinking. Voice the reader's likely objection as the hook itself,
   then answer it.
10. The insider tip. Frame the line as information being shared rather than sold, a tip
    reads as help, a claim reads as ad copy.
11. The short list. Promise a fixed small count, three ways, five signs, a bounded list
    lowers the reading-effort estimate and raises click-through.
12. The before-you pattern interrupt. Open with a command against the expected next
    action, stop scrolling, don't do X, breaks feed autopilot.
13. The proof-first. Open with a credential, a stat, or a name-check before the claim, so
    the claim lands as already substantiated.
14. The specificity swap. Replace a generic noun with an unusually precise one, the
    detail itself signals the claim is real.
15. The stakes-flip. State what happens if nothing changes before stating the offer,
    cost-of-inaction framed ahead of the solution.
